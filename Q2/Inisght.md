Most brands pursue an expansion strategy centered on high franchising rates while accepting low sales per unit. This is shown in the scatterplot, where most brands are located in the second quadrant. The pie chart further supports this observation, showing that franchised units make up more than 80% of all units. This approach is understandable given the diversity of the U.S. market and the intense competition within the fast-food industry. Expanding successfully often requires adapting products, operations, and marketing strategies to local conditions, so franchising is a viable option. 

Notable exceptions include McDonald's, Chick-fil-A, Chipotle, and Panda Express. McDonald's and Chick-fil-A achieve both high sales per unit and high franchising rates. To the other end, Raising Cane's, Chipotle, and Panda Express retain ownership of nearly all their locations (94%–100%), allowing them to maintain tighter operational control while still generating above-average sales per unit. Starbucks presents a different case: despite below-average sales per unit, it achieves exceptionally high total sales through its extensive store network.

In terms of total sales, McDonald's, Starbucks, and Chick-fil-A lead the industry, together accounting for approximately 33% of total sales. Starbucks is the only beverage-focused brand in the top 10, indicating that food-focused concepts may be more profitable than beverage-focused ones, particularly because fast food is commonly consumed as a meal in the United States.

Looking ahead, the fast-food industry will continue to evolve. Regardless of the expansion model chosen, long-term success will depend on two key factors: technology adoption and customer loyalty. Brands should also capitalize on major opportunities created by large-scale events such as the 2026 FIFA World Cup.
